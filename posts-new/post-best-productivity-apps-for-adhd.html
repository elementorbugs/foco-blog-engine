Best Productivity Apps for ADHD: 8 That Actually Help

The best productivity app for ADHD isn't the one with the most features — it's the one you'll actually open. ADHD brains abandon complex systems within 4-6 weeks. The apps that work share three things: low setup friction, external structure that does the executive function for you, and dopamine built into the start. Below: 8 apps that consistently work for ADHD adults, with honest pros and cons for each.

Key Takeaways

  • The "best" ADHD productivity app depends on what specifically you struggle with — task initiation, focus, scheduling, or follow-through.
  • Complex apps (Notion, custom systems) usually fail for ADHD because the setup itself becomes a task initiation problem.
  • The most reliable apps for ADHD focus on one job done well, not a swiss-army knife.
  • FOCO breaks tasks into tiny steps, Goblin Tools sequences them, Tiimo visualizes schedules, Sunsama anchors daily ritual.
  • Paid apps aren't always better. Free apps like Forest and Goblin Tools work well for many ADHD adults.

What makes a productivity app actually good for ADHD?

Most productivity apps are designed by neurotypical people for neurotypical brains. They assume you can plan, prioritize, and follow through if you just have the right framework. ADHD brains can't reliably do those things — and the apps that pretend otherwise fail their ADHD users within weeks.

The apps that actually work for ADHD share three properties:

1. Low setup friction. If you have to spend an hour customizing the app before using it, you won't. The activation cost has to be near zero.

2. External structure. The app does executive function work for you — it suggests next steps, sets reminders, paces you, breaks down tasks. It doesn't ask you to generate that structure yourself.

3. Dopamine at the start, not just the finish. Tiny rewards for beginning, not just completing. Visual progress, satisfying feedback, gentle gamification.

The 8 apps below all check at least 2 of these 3 boxes. They're the ones consistently recommended by ADHD coaches and ADHD adults themselves.

The 8 best productivity apps for ADHD

1. FOCO — Best for task initiation paralysis

What it does: Type any task. FOCO instantly breaks it into 5 tiny first steps with realistic time estimates. The "open the document" first step bypasses the dopamine block that task paralysis creates.

Best for: ADHD adults who freeze on tasks they know they should do. The "I sit down and nothing happens" pattern.

Pros: Zero setup. Instant value from first use. Built specifically for ADHD task initiation — not adapted from a generic productivity tool. Free trial available.

Cons: Doesn't replace a full project management system. Best paired with another tool for long-term tracking.

Try at: tryfoco.com

2. Goblin Tools — Best free task breakdown

What it does: "Magic ToDo" feature breaks tasks into subtasks with an adjustable difficulty slider — the more "spicy" the slider, the smaller the steps. Great for the same problem FOCO solves but with a different design philosophy.

Best for: Anyone who wants free task breakdown and prefers a more minimalist interface.

Pros: Free for core features. Lightweight. Built by an autistic developer for neurodivergent users — the design respects how our brains actually work.

Cons: Less guided onboarding than FOCO. No mobile-native app for some platforms (web-first).

Try at: goblin.tools

3. Tiimo — Best for visual schedules

What it does: Visual day planner originally designed for autism and ADHD. Each task has an icon, a color, and a duration. The visual structure gives time-blind ADHD brains a real sense of how the day is laid out.

Best for: ADHD adults whose biggest struggle is time perception and seeing the day as a whole.

Pros: Beautiful, calming UI. Strong visual structure compensates for time blindness. Co-designed with neurodivergent users.

Cons: Subscription-only (~$10/month). Can feel restrictive if you prefer text-based planning.

Try at: tiimoapp.com

4. Sunsama — Best for daily ritual

What it does: Built around a "daily planning ritual" — every morning you spend 10 minutes choosing what you'll work on, dragging tasks into time slots. The ritual itself is the value.

Best for: Knowledge workers and ADHD adults who can sustain a 10-minute daily commitment in exchange for a less chaotic day.

Pros: Forces realistic daily scoping. Integrates with Slack, Trello, calendar, etc. Strong design.

Cons: ~$20/month. The ritual itself is the friction — if you can't sustain it, the app doesn't help. Many ADHD adults try Sunsama, love the idea, abandon it within a month.

Try at: sunsama.com

5. TickTick — Best general-purpose to-do

What it does: Cross-platform to-do with built-in Pomodoro timer, calendar view, recurring tasks, and natural language input ("Pay rent every 1st").

Best for: ADHD adults who want one app for everything — tasks, focus timer, calendar — without complex setup.

Pros: Free tier is generous. Pomodoro built in. Simple but powerful. Works across all platforms.

Cons: No specific ADHD design considerations. Can become cluttered if you over-use it.

Try at: ticktick.com

6. Forest — Best for focus accountability

What it does: Plant a virtual tree at the start of a focus session. The tree grows during the session and dies if you leave the app. Over time you build a virtual forest. Gentle gamification of focused work blocks.

Best for: ADHD adults whose primary problem is phone distraction during focus blocks.

Pros: Free version works fine. The "killing your tree" friction is surprisingly effective. Real trees planted via partnership for premium users.

Cons: Doesn't help with task initiation — only with sustaining focus once you've started.

Try at: forestapp.cc

7. Notion — Best for custom systems (with caveats)

What it does: Highly customizable workspace where you can build any productivity system imaginable — task lists, databases, wikis, dashboards.

Best for: ADHD adults who genuinely enjoy building systems AND can sustain them. A small minority of ADHD users thrive in Notion. The majority abandon it.

Pros: Infinitely flexible. Free for personal use. Strong template ecosystem.

Cons: The setup IS a task initiation problem. Many ADHD adults spend hours building the perfect Notion system, then never use it. Recommended only if you've successfully sustained a complex tool for 6+ months in the past.

Try at: notion.so

8. Brain.fm — Best for focus audio

What it does: AI-generated music designed specifically for sustained focus. Different from regular instrumental music — explicitly engineered to occupy background attention without engaging cognitive resources.

Best for: ADHD adults who find brown noise effective and want a more pleasant alternative.

Pros: Many users report dramatic focus improvement. Marketed with neuroscience claims (some debatable, but user experience is consistent).

Cons: ~$10/month. Only addresses the focus-during-work part, not task initiation or planning.

Try at: brain.fm

Which ADHD productivity app should you choose?

Pick based on which executive function struggle is biggest for you right now.

If you can't START tasks: FOCO or Goblin Tools. Both target task initiation directly.

If you can't see your day: Tiimo. Visual structure compensates for time blindness.

If you need a daily anchor: Sunsama. The morning ritual is the value.

If you need one app for everything: TickTick. Pomodoro + tasks + calendar in a single tool.

If your problem is phone distraction during work: Forest. Simple, free, effective.

If background sound helps you focus: Brain.fm or free brown noise.

If you've successfully built and sustained complex systems before: Notion.

Don't try to use all 8. Pick 1-2 and commit for at least 4 weeks before judging. ADHD brains often need the novelty of a new tool to wear off before you can tell if it's actually working.

Are paid ADHD productivity apps worth it?

Sometimes — but not always.

The free apps in this list (Goblin Tools, TickTick free tier, Forest, brown noise on YouTube) are powerful enough for many ADHD adults. Don't pay for an app until you've validated that you'll actually use one consistently.

That said, when a paid app addresses your specific weak skill well, it's often money well spent. $10-20/month on an app you use daily is dramatically cheaper than the time, missed opportunities, and stress that come with chronic disorganization.

The honest test: if you're using a paid app for less than 3-4 days per week consistently after 30 days, cancel it. The fit isn't right.

What productivity app features should ADHD adults avoid?

Some features that sound helpful actively hurt ADHD users.

Avoid: Complex tagging systems. "Add 5 tags to every task" is more executive function work, not less. The brain that can't decide what to do first definitely can't decide what to tag it.

Avoid: Long onboarding tutorials. If the app demands 30 minutes of setup before you can use it, you'll never finish. The activation cost is too high.

Avoid: Overly cute or noisy interfaces. Bright colors, excessive animations, achievement notifications — these are themselves distractions for some ADHD brains.

Avoid: Apps that depend on you adding tasks daily. The very act of adding tasks IS a task initiation problem. Look for apps that auto-import from email, calendar, or Slack.

Avoid: Productivity dashboards. Looking at the dashboard becomes a procrastination activity. The best ADHD apps minimize the meta-work of checking your productivity.

FAQ

What's the difference between FOCO and Goblin Tools?

Both break tasks down. FOCO is more guided (gives you 5 specific steps with time estimates and walks you through them) and is mobile-first. Goblin Tools is more flexible (you adjust the breakdown depth via slider) and is web-first. Try both — they have free tiers.

Is there an ADHD app that does everything?

No. Beware any app that claims to. The apps that try to do everything for ADHD typically fail because the breadth itself becomes overwhelming. Pick a tool for your biggest specific problem.

Should I use multiple apps or one?

Most ADHD adults end up with 2-3 apps that each do one job well. A task-initiation tool (FOCO/Goblin), a focus tool (Forest/Brain.fm), and a calendar/planning tool (Tiimo/Sunsama) covers most needs.

Can a productivity app replace ADHD medication?

No. Medication addresses the underlying dopamine system. Apps provide structural support. They work better together than either alone.

How long until I know if an app works?

4 weeks minimum. The first 1-2 weeks are novelty. Real evaluation begins in week 3. If it's still helping at week 6, it's a keeper.

What if I keep abandoning every app?

That's a clue, not a failure. The pattern often means: (a) you're picking apps that don't match your specific weak skill, (b) you're trying complex apps when you need simple ones, or (c) the underlying executive dysfunction needs medical evaluation. None of those are character flaws.

The Bottom Line

The best productivity app for ADHD isn't the one with the most features. It's the one you'll actually open tomorrow.

Pick one — preferably one that addresses your single biggest executive function struggle. Use it daily for 4 weeks. If it sticks, build from there. If it doesn't, try the next one.

You don't need to find the perfect system. You just need to find the next tiny first step.
