The catch is that it forgets everything the moment you close the tab, and breaking a task down still doesn't make you start it. The best alternative depends on what you need next: FOCO to actually begin the first step, Tiimo for visual breakdown that lives somewhere, Saner.AI for dumping a messy brain, or Mind Your Now as the closest all-in-one successor. Here are six, with real pricing and honest trade-offs.

Why look for a Goblin Tools alternative

Let's be fair to Goblin Tools first, because it earned its fans. Magic To-Do is genuinely clever: you paste in something vague and dreadful like "sort out the garage," and it breaks it into small, doable steps. For an ADHD brain staring at an overwhelming blob of a task, that alone can unstick you.

It's also free on the web, runs in your browser, and asks for no account. That's a low bar to entry, and it matters.

So where does it fall short? Three places.

First, it doesn't remember anything. Each tool is a one-off. Close the tab and your broken-down list is gone, so it never becomes the place your tasks actually live.

Second, the tools are separate. Magic To-Do, Formalizer, the Estimator, Judge, they're islands. There's no single home that ties your day together.

Third, and this is the big one: breaking a task into steps does not make you start it. You can have a perfect five-step list and still sit there. That gap, between a tidy plan and actually beginning, is where most ADHD days are lost. It's the task initiation problem, and a smarter list doesn't solve it.

So the right alternative depends on which of those gaps hurts most: keeping your tasks somewhere, tying tools together, or finally starting. The list below is sorted with that in mind.

6 Goblin Tools alternatives for ADHD

1. FOCO, for the part Goblin can't do: starting

Here's the honest pitch. Goblin breaks the task down. FOCO gets you to do the first piece.

Instead of handing you a five-item list to feel guilty about, FOCO puts one step in front of you, sets a short timer, and keeps you company while you work. There's nothing to organize and no day to fall behind on.

Its body doubling is the part worth being precise about: it's AI body doubling, an on-screen focus companion (the Foco character) that stays present and shifts as your session runs. You get the felt sense of working next to someone, with no real people, no video, and no signup.

Best for: people who can break a task down fine but still can't make themselves begin.

Pricing: free to start, with a paid Pro tier.

Honest take: FOCO won't break a vague task into sub-steps for you the way Magic To-Do does. Use Goblin or Tiimo to break it down, then use FOCO to actually start. They pair well.

2. Tiimo, like Magic To-Do, but it remembers

If what you love about Goblin is the breakdown, Tiimo is the closest upgrade. It also uses AI to split tasks into steps, but it's visual, it's built for neurodivergent brains, and crucially it keeps your plan instead of forgetting it.

Color, icons, and visual timers make the day feel concrete rather than abstract, which helps with time blindness.

Best for: people who want Goblin's breakdown plus a calm visual home for it.

Pricing: a free version on mobile, with a paid Pro subscription. We go deeper in our Tiimo alternative guide.

3. Saner.AI, when your brain is a pile of notes

Some of us don't need steps so much as a place to throw the chaos. Saner.AI is built for that: you dump tasks, notes, and half-thoughts, and the AI organizes them without making you build folders or pick categories.

It's the opposite of a rigid system, which is exactly why it suits brains that bounce off structure.

Best for: people who capture in messy bursts and want something to make sense of it after.

Pricing: a free trial, then paid plans.

4. Mind Your Now, the closest all-in-one successor

If your real frustration with Goblin is that the tools are scattered, Mind Your Now pulls the same kind of micro-tools into one place. It adds an AI "compass" that surfaces what's critical now versus what can wait, plus habits and a calendar.

It even plugs into a general AI chat, so the "ask it to break this down" move lives next to your actual tasks.

Best for: people who want Goblin's toolbox feel without the scattered tabs.

Pricing: free to start, with a paid upgrade.

5. Llama Life, to put a clock on each step

Once a task is broken down, Llama Life is good at the next problem: doing the steps without losing two hours to the first one. You give each item a time, and a visible countdown runs while you work.

That gentle pressure is surprisingly effective for ADHD, where an open-ended task quietly expands to fill the whole day.

Best for: people who have the steps but lose track of time inside them.

Pricing: a paid subscription with a free trial.

6. Todoist, the dependable default

Not everyone wants something clever. If you just need a fast, reliable list that syncs everywhere and gets out of your way, Todoist is the safe pick, and its AI assist can break a task down when you ask.

The trap with ADHD is over-building it. Keep one "Today" list, add things as they pop up, and skip due dates unless they're real.

Best for: people who mostly need to stop forgetting things.

Pricing: a genuinely usable free plan; Pro is around $4 a month billed annually.

How to choose

Skip the feature lists and answer one question: where does the task actually die for you?

It dies at "this is too big." You need breakdown. Stay with Goblin, or move to Tiimo so the breakdown sticks.

It dies at "I have the steps but can't start." That's initiation, and no list fixes it. FOCO, or read ADHD task paralysis first.

It dies in a pile of scattered notes. Saner.AI or Mind Your Now to corral the mess.

It dies because one step eats the whole afternoon. Llama Life, or a Pomodoro timer.

FAQ

Are these alternatives free like Goblin Tools?

Some are. FOCO, Tiimo (mobile), Mind Your Now, and Todoist all have free tiers you can use indefinitely. Saner.AI and Llama Life are paid with free trials. Goblin Tools stays free on the web, so a paid app has to clearly do more to be worth it.

What is the best Goblin Tools alternative for breaking tasks down?

Tiimo is the closest, because it also uses AI to split tasks into steps but keeps the result and shows it visually. Saner.AI is good if you'd rather dump everything and let the AI organize it.

I can break tasks down but still can't start. Which tool helps?

That's task initiation, a separate problem from breakdown. FOCO is built for it: it shows one step, starts a timer, and adds an AI focus companion so beginning feels less like a cliff edge.

Is there a good Goblin Tools alternative for autism or AuDHD?

Tiimo was designed specifically for neurodivergent users, including autism and AuDHD, and its visual, low-text approach tends to suit sensory and time-blindness needs well.

Is Goblin Tools still worth using in 2026?

Yes, as a quick breakdown tool. It's free and fast for unsticking an overwhelming task. You just may want to pair it with something that remembers your tasks and helps you start them.

The bottom line

Goblin Tools solves one real problem well: turning a scary, vague task into steps. That's worth keeping in your pocket.

But a list is not a start. If you keep breaking tasks down and still not doing them, the missing piece isn't a better breakdown, it's something that lowers the cost of beginning. For most ADHD brains, that's FOCO, and you can try it free.
