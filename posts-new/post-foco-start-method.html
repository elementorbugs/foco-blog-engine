The FOCO Start Method: How to Start a Task With ADHD

The hardest part of a task with ADHD usually isn't planning it, it's starting it. The FOCO Start Method is a five-step system built for that exact gap: brain-dump the task, shrink it to one tiny first step, start a short timed session, work alongside a body double, and re-enter gently after any interruption. It lowers the activation energy instead of demanding more willpower.

Key Takeaways

  • For ADHD brains, the gap between knowing what to do and actually beginning is the real problem, not laziness or a lack of a plan.
  • The FOCO Start Method targets initiation directly with five steps: brain dump, shrink, short session, body double, gentle re-entry.
  • Each step lowers the activation energy of starting rather than asking for more discipline.
  • The method works with how executive function actually operates, which is why it survives the days a to-do list fails.
  • You can run the whole method in under two minutes of setup, with tools you already have.

Why is starting a task the hardest part with ADHD?

Starting is the hardest part because a task is not one action. It is dozens of tiny decisions, and the ADHD brain stalls before it can pick the first one.

This is task paralysis, the gap between intention and action. You know exactly what to do, you want to do it, and your body still will not move.

The cause is an executive function gap in task initiation, not a character flaw. Planning a task uses different brain systems than starting one, which is why so many ADHD adults can plan endlessly and still never begin.

Most productivity advice ignores this. It assumes that once you have a plan, starting is automatic. The FOCO Start Method is built for the brain where it is not.

What is the FOCO Start Method?

The FOCO Start Method is a five-step sequence designed to lower the activation energy of beginning. Instead of pushing harder, each step removes a specific barrier that keeps you stuck.

The five steps are: brain dump the task, shrink it to one tiny first step, start a short timed session, work alongside a body double, and re-enter gently after an interruption. You can do all five with a piece of paper and a timer.

Here is each step, and why it works.

Step 1: Brain dump the task

Get the task out of your head and onto paper or a screen. Holding a task in working memory while trying to start it doubles the load, and ADHD working memory is already stretched thin.

Write the messy version. Not a clean list, just everything the task involves, in any order. The point is to stop the task from living in your head, where it feels bigger and vaguer than it is.

Once it is external, your brain can stop guarding it and start acting on it.

Step 2: Shrink it to one tiny first step

From the brain dump, pick the single smallest action you could take. Not the task, not the next hour, just the first physical move.

"Write the report" is a wall. "Open the document and type the title" is a step you can actually start. The shrink is the whole game, because the ADHD brain can begin a small, defined action far more easily than a large, vague one.

If the first step still feels heavy, shrink it again. There is no step too small to count.

Step 3: Start a short timed session

Set a timer for 5 to 15 minutes and work only until it rings. A defined endpoint makes the task feel finite, which is exactly what time blindness takes away.

The timer also kills the fear that starting means losing your whole day. You are not committing to finishing, only to a few minutes, and a few minutes is enough to break the freeze. Our guide to the 2-minute rule uses the same principle.

Most of the time, momentum carries you past the timer. When it does not, you still started, and that counts.

Step 4: Work alongside a body double

Starting is easier when you are not alone. Body doubling means doing your task while another person is present and doing theirs, and it measurably lowers the friction of beginning.

Their presence acts as external regulation your brain can borrow when it cannot generate its own. You do not need them to help or even to talk. You just need them there.

The FOCO app is built around this step, pairing a focus session with a body-doubling companion so the hardest moment, actually beginning, happens with backup.

Step 5: Re-enter gently after an interruption

Interruptions do not just pause an ADHD task, they often end it. When focus breaks, the task can vanish from your attention entirely, and getting back in feels like starting from zero again.

So plan for re-entry. When you come back, do not restart the whole method or review everything you missed. Look at your brain dump, pick one tiny step, and start a short session again.

Treat every return as a fresh, small start. Collapsing the gap between where you are and where you "should" be is what keeps the shame spiral from taking the task away for good.

How is this different from a normal to-do list?

A to-do list tells you what to do. The FOCO Start Method helps you begin, which is the part a list leaves out.

Lists assume your brain can take a written task and turn it into action on its own. For ADHD brains, that conversion is exactly where things break. The method adds the missing layer: shrinking, timing, and external support that move you from the page to the first action.

FAQ

How do I start a task when I have ADHD and feel frozen?

Shrink the task to one tiny physical action, like opening the document or putting on your shoes, then set a timer for five minutes and do only that. The freeze breaks when the first step is small enough that your brain stops treating it as a threat.

Why can I plan tasks but not start them with ADHD?

Planning and starting use different parts of executive function. Many ADHD adults can plan in detail and still stall at initiation, because task initiation is a separate skill that ADHD affects directly. It is not a motivation problem.

How long should the first session be?

Start with 5 to 15 minutes. Short enough that your brain can tolerate it, long enough to build momentum. You are aiming to start, not to finish, so the timer should feel almost too easy.

What if I get interrupted halfway through?

Treat the return as a brand new small start. Look at your brain dump, pick one tiny step, and run another short timed session. Do not try to recover the lost momentum all at once, just begin again gently.

The Bottom Line

You do not have a discipline problem. You have an initiation gap, and willpower was never the right tool for it.

The FOCO Start Method works because it makes the first step small enough, finite enough, and supported enough that your brain can actually take it. Brain dump, shrink, start a short session, bring in a body double, and re-enter gently. Then let momentum do the rest.
