The 1/3/5 Rule for ADHD: The Complete Guide

The 1/3/5 rule is a daily task prioritization method where you pick exactly 9 things to do: 1 big task, 3 medium tasks, and 5 small tasks. For ADHD adults, it works because the cap prevents overwhelm, the structure removes decision fatigue, and the mix of sizes gives you steady dopamine hits all day. Below: how to use it, real examples, when it fails, and how to adapt it for an ADHD brain.

Key Takeaways

  • The 1/3/5 rule caps your daily to-do list at 9 items: 1 big, 3 medium, 5 small. That's the whole rule.
  • It works for ADHD because it prevents the infinite-list spiral AND the under-planned 2-item day, both of which fail.
  • The mix of task sizes is the point — small tasks give dopamine, medium tasks give momentum, the big task gives meaning.
  • Most ADHD adults need 2-3 weeks to calibrate what "big" vs "medium" vs "small" actually means for them.
  • It's not a clinical treatment. It's a behavioral scaffold. Pair it with the right tools and it works; rely on it as the whole answer and it won't.

What is the 1/3/5 rule for ADHD?

The 1/3/5 rule is a daily task prioritization method. You pick exactly nine things to do each day: one large task, three medium tasks, and five small tasks. That's it. No exceptions, no add-ons, no "just one more thing."

The technique didn't come from the ADHD world. It was popularized by Alex Cavoulacos of The Muse around 2014 as a productivity hack for anyone overwhelmed by long to-do lists. It got picked up by the ADHD coaching community a few years later because it solves several specific ADHD failure modes at once.

The "1/3/5" part is the breakdown by size. The "rule" part is the cap. Nine items per day. Stop.

You write the list the night before or the first ten minutes of the morning. You do not add to it during the day. When you finish all nine, you're done. When you finish fewer than nine, you carry the rest to tomorrow and adjust.

It looks too simple to work. That's part of why it works.

Why does the 1/3/5 rule work for ADHD brains?

Three mechanisms, all grounded in how ADHD brains actually fail at planning.

The cap kills the infinite list. Most ADHD adults default to one of two broken strategies: write down thirty things and freeze, or write down two critical things and lose track of everything else. Both fail.

Thirty items overwhelms working memory. The Russell Barkley executive function model describes ADHD as fundamentally a problem of self-regulation, and the working memory load of thirty items pushes the prefrontal cortex past its capacity ceiling. You don't start; you just stare. The 1/3/5 rule keeps the list at nine, which sits well within working memory.

The mix prevents dopamine starvation. ADHD brains need novelty and small wins throughout the day to keep dopamine flowing. A list of all-big tasks creates long stretches without a completion signal — you burn out before you finish anything. A list of all-small tasks feels good but produces no real progress, which triggers shame by evening.

The 1/3/5 mix solves both. The five small tasks give you frequent dopamine hits. The three medium tasks build momentum. The one big task gives the day meaning.

The structure removes decision fatigue. Choosing what to do is itself a task that depletes executive function. ADHD adults often spend the first 90 minutes of the day "trying to figure out what to do," which is itself the avoidance. The 1/3/5 structure pre-decides the shape. You only pick the contents, not the format.

According to the National Institute of Mental Health, ADHD adults benefit measurably from external structure that substitutes for the internal self-regulation their brains struggle to generate. CHADD notes that executive function difficulties affect roughly 9 in 10 adults with ADHD — which is exactly the population that external task-limiting systems like 1/3/5 are designed to serve. The 1/3/5 rule is one of the simplest external scaffolds you can build.

What counts as a "big" task vs "medium" vs "small"?

This is the part most people get wrong on day one, and the calibration takes two to three weeks of practice.

A big task is the most important thing of the day. It takes 60 to 180 minutes of focused work. It has clear edges — you know when it's done. Examples: write a project proposal draft, prepare for tomorrow's interview, file the tax return, finish the report introduction.

A big task is not "do my entire project." That's a goal, not a task. The big task is the next 90-minute chunk of the project.

A medium task takes 20 to 60 minutes and requires focus but not deep work. Examples: clear the kitchen, write three follow-up emails, schedule the dentist appointment and pharmacy refill, review the contract changes.

A medium task usually has 2-5 sub-steps. It's bigger than a single action but smaller than a project.

A small task takes under 20 minutes and is essentially one action. Examples: pay the electric bill, send the wire transfer, text the babysitter, take out trash, refill water bottle, schedule one call.

The trap most ADHD adults fall into: classifying everything as "medium" because everything feels medium. If you find yourself doing this, ask: could I finish this in under 20 minutes with zero distractions? If yes, it's small. Will this take more than an hour? If yes, it's big.

Calibration takes time. Most people overestimate "medium" for two weeks before learning their own scale.

How do you actually use the 1/3/5 rule day-to-day?

Five steps. The whole setup takes 10 minutes once you have the rhythm.

  1. Write the list the night before, or the first 10 minutes of the morning. Not mid-day. The list is fixed once you write it. Pick a quiet moment.
  2. Start with the 1 big task. What's the most important thing tomorrow? Just one. The thing that, if you do it, makes the day a win regardless of everything else.
  3. Add the 3 medium tasks. What's the next tier — important but not critical? Pick three. Resist the urge to pick four.
  4. Add the 5 small tasks. Errands, replies, paperwork, anything under 20 minutes. Five total.
  5. Start with the big task in the morning. Highest-energy slot, highest-stakes task. Don't warm up with small tasks — that depletes the morning energy on low-value work.

That's it. Nine items, in approximate order of importance. The whole list fits on a sticky note.

The discipline isn't in the writing. It's in the not-adding. When a tenth task arrives mid-morning, write it on a separate "tomorrow" list. Don't add it to today's nine.

If your problem is even starting the big task, pair the 1/3/5 rule with task initiation tools — the rule tells you what to do, but it doesn't solve the moment you can't start.

Why exactly nine tasks? Why not ten or seven?

Nine is not magic, but it's well-calibrated for working memory and a typical workday.

Working memory holds about 7-9 items reliably for non-ADHD adults, and 2-3 for ADHD adults. Nine items on paper, externalized from working memory, is the upper bound of what feels scannable without re-reading. Ten starts to feel like a list. Seven feels too small.

Nine items distribute across an 8-10 hour workday at roughly the right cadence. One 90-minute big task, three 30-45-minute medium tasks, five sub-20-minute small tasks. That's about 5-7 hours of actual work, leaving room for breaks, transitions, and the inevitable unexpected.

The 1/3/5 split specifically matches the dopamine cycle. Five small wins, three medium wins, one big win — the small wins are frequent enough to keep momentum, the big win is just one so it doesn't fragment focus.

People have proposed variants (1/3/5/7, 2/3/5, 1/3/9) but none of them have caught on. The original ratio works because each tier serves a different psychological function, and changing the numbers breaks the balance.

If you're struggling, try the 0/2/3 version on hard days — zero big tasks, two medium, three small. Five items total. It's the same principle scaled down.

What if you don't finish them all?

This is the question that decides whether the rule works for you or destroys you.

If you treat unfinished tasks as failures, the rule will become another source of shame on top of the rest. ADHD adults are especially vulnerable to this — there's a long history of "productivity systems" that punish you for being yourself.

The honest framing: 1/3/5 is calibration, not commitment. If you finished four items today, you over-planned. Adjust tomorrow. If you finished all nine plus three extras, you under-planned. Adjust tomorrow.

Most ADHD adults finish 5-7 of the 9 items in the first month. That's normal. The data is the point — at month two, you start writing more realistic lists.

The carry-forward rule: anything you didn't finish gets re-considered for tomorrow, not automatically added.

Maybe it was wrong to put on today's list. Maybe it needs to be smaller, or deleted. Don't just shuffle it forward.

One common failure: the same big task carries forward five days in a row. That means the task is wrong — too big, too vague, or you're avoiding it for a reason you haven't named yet. Break it down further or pause and ask what's actually blocking it.

Can you adapt the 1/3/5 rule for high-stress days?

Yes, and you should. Rigid adherence to any system is itself an ADHD trap.

Crisis day version: 1/0/0. When something has gone sideways — a sick kid, a work emergency, an ADHD crash day — the rule collapses to one task. Whatever the crisis actually requires. Skip everything else. Don't perform productivity on top of dealing with a real thing.

Low-energy version: 0/0/3. Three small tasks, nothing else. This is the version for the days where executive function is barely online. The goal is to feel like you did something, even if it's just paying the bill, sending the email, and unloading the dishwasher.

High-focus version: 1/3/0. One big, three medium, zero small. Used on rare days when you have a clear runway for deep work and don't want to fragment focus with small task switching. The small tasks roll to tomorrow.

Hyperfocus catchup version: 1/5/5. Used when you're emerging from a long stretch of unproductivity and need to clear backlog. More medium tasks than usual, because catching up is mostly medium-sized cleanup. Use sparingly — it's tempting to make this your default and it'll burn you out.

The pattern: adapt the numbers to your current capacity, but keep the principle of pre-committed limits.

How does the 1/3/5 rule compare to other ADHD productivity systems?

The 1/3/5 rule occupies a specific niche. Here's how it fits next to neighbors.

The 1/3/5 rule pairs well with most of these, especially the 2-minute rule and Pomodoro. They solve different problems: 1/3/5 decides what, Pomodoro decides how long, and the 2-minute rule decides do or defer.

The 1/3/5 rule competes most directly with the Eisenhower matrix and basic to-do lists. It beats both for ADHD adults because it pre-commits the structure, where Eisenhower asks you to do executive function work to sort things.

When does the 1/3/5 rule fail for ADHD adults?

Three failure modes, all worth naming upfront.

The big task is wrong. If your big task is "finish the project," you'll fail every day for a week. "Finish" is not a task, it's an outcome. The big task should be a 60-180 minute concrete unit of work: "draft the introduction," "review the contract," "build the slide deck." If you keep failing on the big task, the task itself is broken.

You add tasks during the day. The cap is the rule. If you let yourself add a tenth, you'll add an eleventh, and by week two you're back to an infinite list. New tasks that arrive mid-day go on tomorrow's list, not today's. If something is truly urgent, it replaces an existing item, not adds to the list.

You use it as moral self-evaluation. If finishing 4 of 9 becomes "I'm a failure" instead of "I miscalibrated, adjust tomorrow," the rule becomes part of the shame spiral that ADHD adults already manage too much of. The numbers are data, not judgment.

A fourth, quieter failure: the rule becomes the goal. Some people optimize for "did I complete my 1/3/5 list?" instead of "did I do the right work?" The list is a tool. The work is the work.

What's a real example of a 1/3/5 day?

Here's an actual Wednesday from an ADHD professional, names changed.

1 BIG (90 min):
☐ Draft Q3 strategy document — introduction + first two sections

3 MEDIUM (~40 min each):
☐ Email Sarah back about partnership terms (with revised contract notes)
☐ Prep slides for Thursday 2pm client call
☐ Tax receipts — file Jan-Mar in Drive, scan paper ones

5 SMALL (~10-15 min each):
☐ Pay electric bill (overdue)
☐ Schedule dentist for next week
☐ Reply to Mom's text about Saturday
☐ Cancel old subscription (the one I keep forgetting)
☐ Refill prescription via app

That's it. Nine items. Should take roughly 6 hours of actual work, leaving room for the meetings already on the calendar, transitions, lunch, and the unexpected.

Notice what's not there: "be more focused," "stop procrastinating," "fix my routine." Those are not tasks. They're abstractions, and they belong in a coaching conversation, not a to-do list.

Notice also: the big task is one specific chunk of a larger project, not the project itself. The medium tasks have clear edges. The small tasks are single actions.

How do you write down a 1/3/5 list?

Anywhere visible, in a fixed format, with the date.

The format matters because the structure does the cognitive work. Don't reinvent it daily. Three blocks: 1 line, 3 lines, 5 lines. Same order every day.

Paper sticky note works for many ADHD adults. It's tactile, can't be ignored, can't accidentally have more items added (no room). Stick it on the monitor or notebook. Fold and trash at end of day.

A notes app works if you have one you actually open. The risk is that digital lists are easy to silently expand. Use a template that physically only has 9 lines.

A bullet journal works if you already keep one. It naturally limits the space.

A whiteboard in your workspace works well for visual learners and people who work from home.

What doesn't work: a generic todo app where you can keep adding items. The cap is the rule, and apps without a cap will let you break the rule silently. If you use a digital tool, use one that respects the limit.

The list is visible all day. You cross items off as you finish them. The visible-progress part matters — ADHD brains need to see the work disappearing or the gain doesn't register.

What if you keep adding tasks during the day?

This is the most common rule break, and it has a specific fix.

Keep a separate "tomorrow" capture page nearby — paper, app, whatever's adjacent. Every time a new task surfaces during the day, write it on the capture page. Do not look at it again until tomorrow morning's planning session.

This works because the urge to add tasks is usually about not losing track, not about actually needing to do it today. Writing it down satisfies the loss-prevention urge. The brain stops nagging.

The capture page also doubles as brain dump material for tomorrow's planning. By morning, you have a pool of pre-captured candidates for the next 1/3/5 list.

Genuinely urgent things (something is on fire) can replace an existing item. Pick one of today's items to demote to tomorrow, and slot the urgent thing in its place. The cap stays at 9.

If you're constantly fighting the urge to add, that's a signal — your tomorrow planning is too sparse and you're trying to compensate mid-day. Spend more time on the night-before list. Make tomorrow's plan feel complete enough that mid-day additions feel unnecessary.

Should you use 1/3/5 with ADHD medication or alone?

Both. The 1/3/5 rule and medication address different parts of the same problem and work better together.

Medication addresses the neurochemistry — dopamine and norepinephrine availability in the prefrontal cortex. It lifts your ceiling of what's possible. But medication doesn't tell you what to do. ADHD adults on stimulants who don't have a planning system often report doing the wrong things very efficiently.

The 1/3/5 rule addresses the planning gap. It tells you what to do, in what order, with what shape. But it doesn't generate the cognitive horsepower to do them. Without medication (if medication is appropriate for you), the 1/3/5 list can still feel like climbing a wall.

Together: medication gives you the energy, 1/3/5 gives you the direction.

If medication isn't part of your treatment, the 1/3/5 rule still works — just expect to use the lower-capacity variants (0/2/3 or 1/3/0) more often. The pattern still holds.

This is not medical advice. Medication decisions are between you and a clinician with experience in adult ADHD.

How is 1/3/5 different from time blocking?

Time blocking assigns specific time slots to tasks. 9-10am: write strategy doc. 10-10:30am: emails. 10:30-11:30am: prep slides.

The 1/3/5 rule assigns tasks to a day without specific time slots. You decide order roughly (big in the morning, small in the afternoon), but you don't pre-allocate exact times.

For ADHD adults, the difference is significant. Time blocking is precise but brittle — if 9-10am doesn't happen (kid woke up sick, your brain wasn't online yet), the whole cascade breaks. Task paralysis often gets worse, not better, under tight time blocking.

1/3/5 is more forgiving. The list is the list, the order is loose. If the big task happens at 10am or 2pm, it still got done. The structure protects against overwhelm without micromanaging your day.

Most ADHD adults do better with 1/3/5 than with strict time blocking. Some hybrid users do time blocking for the big task only, and let the medium and small tasks float — that's a reasonable middle.

If you've tried time blocking and it didn't stick, that's data, not failure. The 1/3/5 rule was designed for the kind of brain that time blocking doesn't fit.

FAQ

Is the 1/3/5 rule scientifically proven for ADHD?

No. The 1/3/5 rule is a behavioral strategy, not a clinically validated ADHD treatment. It works for many ADHD adults because it aligns with what we know about working memory limits and dopamine cycling, but it has not been studied in controlled trials. Treat it as a useful tool, not a treatment.

Do you do the 1/3/5 list before bed or in the morning?

Either works, but most ADHD adults do better with night-before planning. The cognitive load of "deciding the day" is heavy in the morning when executive function is just waking up. Doing it the night before — even just 5 minutes — protects the morning for actual work.

What if my "big task" takes more than one day?

Then your big task is wrong. Break it into a unit that fits in one day's deep-work window (60-180 minutes). "Write the report" is not a task; "write the report's introduction" is. The big task should have a clear finish line within a single day.

Can I do 1/3/5 if I work an unpredictable job?

Yes, with adjustments. If unpredictable interruptions are normal, plan a thinner list (0/2/3 or 1/2/3) so disruptions don't blow up the day. The cap principle still applies — pre-commit to less, not more, and let the day's chaos be expected.

Should I share my 1/3/5 list with my partner or boss?

Optional, but external accountability helps some ADHD adults. Sharing the list with one person who won't shame you for unfinished items can add gentle accountability. Don't share it with anyone who'll use it against you.

What if my list has no "big" task most days?

That's fine for chunks of the year. Maintenance weeks, recovery weeks, and admin-heavy days legitimately don't have one. Run 0/3/5 or 0/4/5 on those days. Don't fabricate a big task to fill the slot.

Is the 1/3/5 rule the same as the 1-3-5 rule?

Yes. The "1/3/5" and "1-3-5" notations refer to the same method. Some people add hyphens, some slashes, but the rule is identical: one big, three medium, five small.

How long does it take to see if 1/3/5 works for me?

Three to four weeks of consistent use. The first week is usually a calibration disaster — your big/medium/small categories will be off. By week 2-3 you've learned your own scale, and by week 4 the rhythm starts to feel natural. Don't quit after three bad days.

The Bottom Line

The 1/3/5 rule works because it limits choice in a brain that suffers from too much of it. One big task, three medium, five small, nine total, capped.

It's not magic. It won't replace medication, therapy, or actual rest. But for the question of what should I do today and how do I keep the list from eating me, it's one of the cleanest answers an ADHD brain can use.

If you're stuck on the big task itself — the moment when you know what to do and still can't start — the 1/3/5 rule won't solve that. That's task initiation deficit, and it needs a different tool. Body doubling or the FOCO app can help bridge the gap from list to action.

Try it tomorrow. One big, three medium, five small.

See what happens, calibrate the next day. Three weeks from now, you'll know if it fits.
