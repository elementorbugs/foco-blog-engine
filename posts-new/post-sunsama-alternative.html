Why look for a Sunsama alternative if you have ADHD?

Sunsama is genuinely well designed. The problem is what it asks of you before you can work.

It opens with a daily planning ritual: review yesterday, pull tasks from your other tools, assign each one a time block, estimate durations, write a focus statement.

For a neurotypical brain, that ceremony creates calm. For an ADHD brain, it often becomes the task that stops you from doing the task.

Research links ADHD to weaker task initiation, the executive-function step between knowing what to do and actually starting. Asking the least reliable part of your brain to plan tomorrow before today is done spends your activation energy on setup.

Cost compounds the friction. Sunsama runs $22 a month (or $17 a month billed annually) after a free trial, with no free tier. That is a real commitment for a tool many ADHD users skip after a few days, then feel guilty about.

The fix is not a better planner. It is a tool that reduces the distance between "I should start" and "I am starting." That is the lens for every pick below.

The 9 best Sunsama alternatives for ADHD

1. FOCO, best for task initiation and body doubling

FOCO removes the planning layer entirely. You pick one task, set a timer, and join a quiet co-working room with other people who are also working. That is the whole flow.

It is built around two mechanisms research ties to ADHD task initiation: body doubling and short, timed sessions. Sessions start at 10 minutes, short enough that your brain does not reject them as overwhelming.

Best for: people whose barrier is starting, not scheduling, and who feel worse, not better, after a daily planning ritual.

Key features: one-task focus mode, live body-doubling rooms, 10 to 50 minute timed sessions, no daily review or backlog.

Pricing: free plan to start, with a paid Pro subscription.

The ADHD trade-off: FOCO is deliberately not a full calendar or project manager. If you also need heavy scheduling, pair it with one of the tools below.

2. Tiimo, best for visual structure and routines

Tiimo is a visual daily planner built specifically for neurodivergent brains. It uses color, icons, and visual timers to make a plan feel concrete instead of abstract.

It is gentler than Sunsama's text-heavy review, which suits people who think in pictures and need routines they can see.

Best for: visual planners who want structure and reminders without a written reflection step.

Pricing: a free version is available on mobile, with a paid Pro subscription for full features.

If you are weighing FOCO against Tiimo specifically, we cover that in detail in our Tiimo alternative guide.

3. Motion, best for AI auto-scheduling

Motion uses AI to build your schedule for you. You add tasks with deadlines and durations, and it slots them around your calendar automatically, rescheduling when things move.

For ADHD, the appeal is that the planning decision is taken off your plate. The catch is that it still expects accurate time estimates, which can be a weak spot.

Best for: people who want software to decide what to do next instead of planning it themselves.

Pricing: no permanent free plan, around $19 per seat per month on the cheaper annual billing, after a free trial.

4. Structured, best for a visual day timeline

Structured lays your day out as a single visual timeline. You drag tasks onto it and see your day as a clear sequence, without Sunsama's guided review.

It is faster to set up and lighter on ceremony, which lowers the activation cost of planning.

Best for: people who want to see the shape of their day at a glance.

Pricing: a free tier with a paid Pro subscription for advanced features.

5. Reclaim.ai, best for automatic time-blocking

Reclaim.ai defends time on your calendar automatically. It schedules focus time, habits, and breaks around your existing meetings, so the calendar fills itself in.

Like Motion, it removes the manual planning step that trips up ADHD brains, while keeping a real calendar at the center.

Best for: calendar-driven people who want focus time protected without doing it by hand.

Pricing: a free Lite plan, with paid plans starting around $10 per seat per month.

6. Todoist, best for simple capture

Todoist is a fast, no-friction task list. For ADHD, its strength is capture: getting a thought out of your head before it disappears.

It works best when you resist building elaborate projects. Keep one "Today" list, add tasks as they occur, and skip due dates unless they are real deadlines.

Best for: people whose problem is forgetting tasks, not starting them.

Pricing: a genuinely useful free plan, with Pro from roughly $4 per month billed annually.

7. TickTick, best for tasks plus habits

TickTick combines a task manager, calendar, and habit tracker in one app, with a built-in Pomodoro timer. For ADHD, having capture and a focus timer together reduces app-switching.

Best for: people who want tasks, habits, and a Pomodoro timer in a single low-cost app.

Pricing: a strong free plan, with Premium at a low yearly fee (around $36 per year).

8. Akiflow, best for consolidating tools

Akiflow pulls tasks from your other apps and your calendar into one command bar, so you plan from a single place instead of five.

It reduces tool-switching, but it is power-user software with its own learning curve, which can be a lot for an ADHD brain on a bad day.

Best for: people drowning in scattered tools who want one inbox.

Pricing: no free plan, around $19 per month billed annually, after a free trial.

9. Habitica, best for gamified motivation

Habitica turns tasks and habits into a role-playing game. You earn points and level up a character by completing tasks, which can supply the dopamine hit that ADHD initiation often lacks.

Best for: people who respond to game mechanics, rewards, and novelty.

Pricing: free and open-source, with an optional low-cost subscription for cosmetic extras.

How to choose by your ADHD bottleneck

The right alternative depends on which part of your day actually breaks down.

If you cannot start: the breakdown is initiation. You need accountability and a tiny first step, so look at FOCO, a Pomodoro timer, or body doubling.

If you cannot decide what to do: let AI schedule it. Motion or Reclaim.ai remove the planning decision.

If you forget tasks: you need capture. Todoist or TickTick keep the load out of your head.

If you lose motivation: add reward and novelty with Habitica, or visual structure with Tiimo or Structured.

Sunsama tries to solve all of these at once, which is exactly why it can feel like too much. For more on the underlying pattern, see our guides to ADHD task paralysis and how to focus with ADHD, plus our roundup of the best ADHD apps.

FAQ

Is Sunsama worth $22 a month?

If you reliably complete its daily planning ritual and value calendar plus task sync in one view, many users find it worth the price. If you have ADHD and keep skipping the daily review, you are paying for a feature you do not use, and a free initiation tool may serve you better.

What is the best Sunsama alternative for ADHD?

For most ADHD users whose barrier is starting, FOCO is the closest fit, because it replaces planning with one-tap initiation and body doubling and has a free plan. If your barrier is deciding what to do, Motion or Reclaim.ai are better.

Is there a free Sunsama alternative?

Yes. Sunsama itself has no free tier, but FOCO, Todoist, TickTick, Structured, Reclaim.ai (Lite), and Habitica all offer free plans you can use indefinitely.

What is the best AI alternative to Sunsama?

Motion and Reclaim.ai are the leading AI options. Both auto-build your schedule so you skip the manual planning step, which is often the part ADHD brains find hardest.

Does Sunsama have a free plan?

No. Sunsama offers a free trial but no permanent free tier. After the trial it is a paid subscription, around $22 per month or $17 per month billed annually.

The bottom line

Sunsama is a strong planner for brains that can hold a plan in working memory and execute it. ADHD is not that brain's strong suit.

If planning is the part that stops you, the answer is not a better planner. It is a tool that shrinks the first step and adds external accountability, then gets out of the way.

That is what FOCO is built to do, and you can start free. Match the tool to your actual bottleneck, not to the one with the most features.
