ADHD Glossary: 25 Terms That Explain What's Happening

If you've ever Googled an ADHD term and gotten three paragraphs of jargon when you wanted a real answer, this is for you. 25 of the most important ADHD concepts, defined clearly, in plain language. Each one links to a deeper guide on FOCO.

Key Takeaways

  • Most ADHD struggles have specific names — once you have the name, you can find the fix.
  • You are not alone in any of these. Each term describes a documented experience, not a personal failure.
  • If a term resonates, click through. The full guide explains the why and what to do.

ADHD Shutdown

A state where executive function collapses entirely. You can't think, choose, or act. Often triggered by overwhelm, sensory overload, or long stretches of understimulation. Different from sleepiness — it's the brain going offline.

Why it matters: Recognizing a shutdown helps you respond with rest instead of self-criticism.

ADHD Tax

The financial and time cost of having ADHD: late fees, missed appointments, replaced lost items, expedited shipping, gym memberships you don't use, food that goes bad in the fridge. It adds up to thousands of dollars a year for most ADHD adults.

Why it matters: Naming it helps you stop treating it as personal failure. ADDitude has a deeper write-up.

Body Doubling

The practice of working alongside another person — physically or in person, on a video call, or in a coffee shop — while you each do your own task. Their presence acts as external regulation that an ADHD brain can borrow. Free, often the most effective non-medication tool.

Why it matters: It addresses three executive function gaps at once. Full guide here.

Co-regulation

When one person's nervous system synchronizes with another's, leading to calmer focus and easier action. It's the mechanism behind why body doubling works. Babies learn it from caregivers; adults benefit from it lifelong.

Why it matters: If self-regulation is hard for you, co-regulation isn't a workaround — it's a feature. More on body doubling.

Decision Paralysis

Being unable to choose between options — even small ones — when each has some merit, often resulting in choosing nothing. Common in ADHD because of working memory load and time blindness compounding.

Why it matters: Reducing the number of decisions you have to make in a day is one of the most underrated ADHD interventions. Related: task paralysis.

Dopamine Deficit

The reduced availability of dopamine in ADHD brains, particularly in regions responsible for motivation and reward. Documented in brain imaging studies. It's the biological reason boring tasks feel impossible to start.

Why it matters: It's why "just try harder" never works. Why task initiation breaks.

Emotional Regulation

The ability to manage the intensity and duration of your emotions. ADHD impairs this — emotions arrive faster, hit harder, and last longer. Small slights can feel devastating. Frustration can become rage in seconds.

Why it matters: It's why ADHD relationships, jobs, and self-image take a beating that has nothing to do with character.

Executive Dysfunction

When one or more executive functions don't work reliably. Common symptoms: trouble starting tasks, finishing tasks, switching tasks, regulating emotions, holding information in mind. It's the broader category that includes task initiation deficit.

Why it matters: It's the umbrella under which ADHD, autism, depression, and burnout all sit. Related: task initiation deficit.

Executive Function

The brain's "management" system: the seven core skills you use to plan, start, focus, monitor, and finish what you set out to do. ADHD impairs all seven, but task initiation and working memory are usually the first to break down.

Why it matters: Once you understand executive function, you understand that most ADHD struggles aren't moral failures.

Hyperfocus

The opposite of distraction. Intense, immersive concentration on a task — often for hours — with reduced awareness of time, hunger, and surroundings. The same dopamine system that fails task paralysis can power hyperfocus on something interesting.

Why it matters: It's why "you can focus when you want to" is a misunderstanding. You can hyperfocus on what your brain finds rewarding — the dopamine doesn't show up on command.

Inhibition

The executive function that lets you NOT do things — not interrupt, not blurt, not act on the first impulse. ADHD impairs inhibition, which is why interrupting, impulsive purchases, and saying things you immediately regret are so common.

Why it matters: It's not poor manners — it's the brain not braking.

Late Diagnosis

Being diagnosed with ADHD as an adult, often after years of struggle, mislabeling as lazy or anxious, or burning out one too many times. Especially common in women, who are systematically underdiagnosed in childhood.

Why it matters: If you're recently diagnosed, the grief is real. So is the relief of finally having a name for it.

Masking

Consciously hiding ADHD traits to fit in: forcing stillness, suppressing stimming, mimicking neurotypical behavior, working twice as hard to look like everyone else. Effective short-term. Exhausting long-term. Often a path to burnout.

Why it matters: Most late-diagnosed ADHD adults masked for years without realizing what they were doing.

Neurodivergent

A descriptor for brains that develop and function differently from the typical norm — including ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia, and more. Coined as a non-pathologizing alternative to "disordered."

Why it matters: Using "neurodivergent" reframes ADHD as a difference rather than a defect.

Perfectionism Paralysis

Being unable to start because you can't guarantee the result will be perfect. Especially common in ADHD because intense self-criticism plus executive dysfunction equals "if I can't do it right, I won't do it at all."

Why it matters: Lowering the bar is often the unlock — done badly beats not done. Related: procrastination vs paralysis.

The Pomodoro Technique

A focus method of 25-minute work blocks separated by 5-minute breaks. Used widely for ADHD because the bounded time horizon reduces overwhelm and the structure provides external pacing.

Why it matters: It's a sustain tool. Pair it with a launch tool like the 2-minute rule.

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)

Extreme emotional pain triggered by perceived rejection, criticism, or failure. Different from "being too sensitive" — it's a neurological response that can leave you reeling for days from a comment most people would forget in minutes.

Why it matters: Up to 99% of people with ADHD experience some form of RSD, according to clinical observations summarized by ADDitude magazine.

Self-Regulation

The brain's ability to manage your own behavior, emotions, and attention without external prompting. Russell Barkley's framework places ADHD as fundamentally a self-regulation disorder.

Why it matters: External tools (apps, body doubling, schedules) work because they replace internal regulation that ADHD brains struggle to generate.

Stimming

Self-stimulatory behavior: fidgeting, tapping, rocking, humming, doodling. Often misread as distractibility, but stimming usually helps ADHD brains regulate and focus, not the opposite.

Why it matters: If you stim, it's probably helping you. Suppressing it costs you something.

Sustained Attention

The ability to maintain focus on something for an extended time, especially when it isn't intrinsically rewarding. ADHD makes this enormously variable: 5 minutes for boring tasks, 5 hours for novel ones.

Why it matters: Once you know your sustained attention is biological, you stop demanding the impossible from yourself.

Task Initiation Deficit

A measurable executive function failure where your brain can't get from "I should do this" to "I'm doing it," even when you genuinely want to. It's not laziness — it's a documented neurological pattern, most often part of ADHD.

Why it matters: It's the clinical name for what most ADHD adults experience as task paralysis. Full explainer here.

Task Paralysis

The acute experience of being frozen, unable to start a task even when there are no obstacles. Your body feels heavy. Your thoughts spiral. The harder you try to push yourself, the more stuck you get.

Why it matters: It's the moment most ADHD adults Google for help — and the central problem FOCO solves. Pillar guide here.

Time Blindness

The ADHD difficulty in perceiving time accurately. A 30-minute task can feel like a 3-hour ordeal. "Five more minutes" can become two hours. The internal clock runs differently from a neurotypical brain's.

Why it matters: It's why deadlines hit late, meetings start by surprise, and "I have plenty of time" so often turns into "I'm completely out of time."

The 2-Minute Rule

A productivity technique that says: shrink your first action until you can do it in under two minutes. For ADHD specifically, it works because it bypasses the dopamine launch your brain can't generate on demand.

Why it matters: It's the closest thing to a reliable unlock most ADHD adults find. Full guide here.

Working Memory

The mental scratchpad your brain uses to hold information in active use. ADHD reduces working memory capacity — which is why instructions slip, you walk into rooms and forget why, and large tasks feel impossibly heavy.

Why it matters: When you "forget" what you were doing, this is usually why. Externalizing tasks (paper, apps) compensates for it directly.

The Bottom Line

If something on this list described you, you're not alone in it. Each of these terms exists because thousands of researchers, clinicians, and ADHD adults named what they were experiencing, again and again, until the experience had a word for it.

You are not lazy. You are not unmotivated. You have a brain that needs different tools — and now you know what to call them.
