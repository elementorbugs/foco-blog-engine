The 5 ADHD Meal-Planning Rules

Meal planning fails ADHD brains when it asks for fresh decisions and effort you've already spent. These rules remove both.

1. Decide once, not daily.

"What should I eat?" every day is dozens of decisions you don't have to spare. Pick from a short set list instead of deciding fresh.

2. Safe foods count.

The same five meals on repeat is a working system, not a failure. Variety is optional. Eating is the goal.

3. Good enough is the bar.

Frozen, pre-cut, and convenience foods are valid. A fed body beats a perfect plan you never cook.

4. Eat on a timer.

Time blindness means you forget to eat until you're running on empty. Set meal cues like alarms, not hunger.

5. Plan for the low days.

Keep a no-cook list for the days you have nothing left. Future-you in a slump needs an option, not a guilt trip.

Fewer decisions. More fed days.

Running on empty

Forgot to Eat Again?

When you suddenly realize it's 3pm and you haven't eaten, skip the plan. Just get fuel in, fast.

It doesn't need to be a real meal. It needs to be something, now. Optimize later, eat first.

Grab-and-go fuel (no cooking)

  • A banana or apple plus a handful of nuts
  • Cheese stick, crackers, whatever's fast
  • A glass of water first, you may be thirsty, not hungry
  • A protein bar or yogurt from the safe-food stash
  • Last night's leftovers, cold is allowed
Set three meal alarms today. A repeating cue beats relying on hunger signals your brain doesn't reliably send.
